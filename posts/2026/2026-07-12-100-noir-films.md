# 100 Noir Films: From Canonical Classics to One-Seeder Obscurities

1. ***The Killing*** *(1956, Stanley Kubrick)* — Scamcore’s ur-text: perfect operational design, unreliable personnel, and money finally reduced to windblown paper.

2. ***Force of Evil*** *(1948, Abraham Polonsky)* — Wall Street folds the numbers racket into a monopoly; crime behaves exactly like respectable capitalism.

3. ***Sweet Smell of Success*** *(1957, Alexander Mackendrick)* — Reputational murder conducted through favors, access, placement, and language: Mamet before Mamet.

4. ***The Asphalt Jungle*** *(1950, John Huston)* — The heist treated as a supply chain in which every specialist brings a private liability.

5. ***Night and the City*** *(1950, Jules Dassin)* — A promoter attempts to arbitrage London’s wrestling underworld without owning a single durable position.

6. ***Double Indemnity*** *(1944, Billy Wilder)* — Insurance procedure becomes both the murder weapon and the intelligence that solves the murder.

7. ***Touch of Evil*** *(1958, Orson Welles)* — Border coordinates, competing jurisdictions, planted evidence, and a policeman who thinks intuition outranks law.

8. ***The Big Heat*** *(1953, Fritz Lang)* — Police, municipal government, and organized crime revealed as departments of the same civic corporation.

9. ***Ace in the Hole*** *(1951, Billy Wilder)* — A reporter manufactures scarcity around a human disaster and builds an extractive economy atop it.

10. ***The Third Man*** *(1949, Carol Reed)* — Occupation bureaucracy creates the black market it condemns; friendship becomes a jurisdictional problem.

11. ***Pickup on South Street*** *(1953, Samuel Fuller)* — Stolen information moves through a street economy where patriotism is merely another bidder.

12. ***Criss Cross*** *(1949, Robert Siodmak)* — An armored-car robbery devised as an alibi for erotic obsession, with its operational logic poisoned at conception.

13. ***The Set-Up*** *(1949, Robert Wise)* — A fixed fight unfolds in real time while everyone except the boxer understands the actual transaction.

14. ***Nightmare Alley*** *(1947, Edmund Goulding)* — Carnival cold reading becomes luxury spiritualism; the grift changes wardrobe but never ontology.

15. ***Odds Against Tomorrow*** *(1959, Robert Wise)* — A bank job whose personnel problem is racism, engineered into mutually assured ruin.

16. ***Out of the Past*** *(1947, Jacques Tourneur)* — Every past agreement returns as a counteroffer, and every apparent exit belongs to somebody else.

17. ***The Killers*** *(1946, Robert Siodmak)* — Opens after doom has won, then lets an insurance investigator audit the payroll robbery that produced it.

18. ***High and Low*** *(1963, Akira Kurosawa)* — Corporate leverage becomes ransom logistics, then class geography, then a procedural descent through the city.

19. ***Rififi*** *(1955, Jules Dassin)* — Criminal labor rendered with absolute procedural rigor, followed by the human cost omitted from the plan.

20. ***Gilda*** *(1946, Charles Vidor)* — A casino operating as a private state, with erotic allegiance, capital, and sovereignty under one roof.

21. ***Bob le flambeur*** *(1956, Jean-Pierre Melville)* — An aging gambler plans a casino robbery while luck and police friendship move on independent tracks.

22. ***Kiss Me Deadly*** *(1955, Robert Aldrich)* — A private detective converts every relationship into an extractive lead and discovers the terminal commodity.

23. ***Scarlet Street*** *(1945, Fritz Lang)* — Authorship fraud, romantic blackmail, and counterfeit success consume a man who cannot claim his own work.

24. ***The Lady from Shanghai*** *(1947, Orson Welles)* — Lawyers and lovers draft one another into competing cons until identity itself becomes inadmissible.

25. ***The Maltese Falcon*** *(1941, John Huston)* — The priceless object is worthless; its market survives because every participant needs the others to believe.

26. ***The Big Sleep*** *(1946, Howard Hawks)* — Plot opacity becomes the point: corruption is a network no individual can fully diagram.

27. ***Gun Crazy*** *(1950, Joseph H. Lewis)* — Crime as an erotic production system, filmed with a physical immediacy decades ahead of studio convention.

28. ***In a Lonely Place*** *(1950, Nicholas Ray)* — Noir turns suspicion inward until love, authorship, violence, and interpretation become indistinguishable.

29. ***White Heat*** *(1949, Raoul Walsh)* — An undercover operative enters a criminal family firm whose command structure is simultaneously maternal and psychotic.

30. ***Detour*** *(1945, Edgar G. Ulmer)* — The entire film may be a murderer’s poverty-row confidence trick performed directly upon the spectator.

31. ***Sunset Boulevard*** *(1950, Billy Wilder)* — Hollywood patronage becomes captivity; the writer sells his agency before realizing it was the deliverable.

32. ***Le samouraï*** *(1967, Jean-Pierre Melville)* — Contract killing purified into procedure, then undone by the nearly invisible residue of human attachment.

33. ***Elevator to the Gallows*** *(1958, Louis Malle)* — An immaculate plan fails because elevators, ropes, cameras, traffic, and chance refuse abstraction.

34. ***D.O.A.*** *(1950, Rudolph Maté)* — A poisoned accountant conducts a forensic audit of his own death before the clock closes the books.

35. ***Laura*** *(1944, Otto Preminger)* — A columnist constructs a woman as an image and mistakes control over her public meaning for ownership of her life.

---

This is the real one-seeder section: forgotten national traditions, poverty-row ontological traps, postwar black markets, and procedural films nobody placed inside the noir lineage.

36. ***The Argyle Secrets*** *(1948, Cy Endfield, USA)* — Its MacGuffin is a dossier connecting respectable American elites to wartime fascism; exposition feels like contraband.

37. ***The Gangster*** *(1947, Gordon Wiles, USA)* — Daniel Fuchs’s diseased interior monologue turns a racketeer into a failed author narrating his own obsolescence.

38. ***Strange Illusion*** *(1945, Edgar G. Ulmer, USA)* — *Hamlet* rebuilt as a PRC suburban nightmare, with cheap sets transformed into chambers of adolescent suspicion and erotic disgust.

39. ***Fear*** *(1946, Alfred Zeisler, USA)* — *Crime and Punishment* compressed into poverty-row delirium; conscience becomes a bad dream whose physical world keeps shrinking.

40. ***Shakedown*** *(1950, Joseph Pevney, USA)* — A photographer manufactures crimes so he can photograph them, converting the camera from witness into extortion apparatus.

41. ***The Second Woman*** *(1950, James V. Kern, USA)* — A modernist house becomes an instrument of gaslighting; architecture behaves like its owner’s guilty, sabotaged mind.

42. ***The Scarf*** *(1951, E. A. Dupont, USA)* — An escaped mental patient cannot determine whether he committed murder; a desert commune offers suspended judgment rather than refuge.

43. ***The Tattooed Stranger*** *(1950, Edward Montagne, USA)* — A weed, a tattoo, and an unidentified corpse turn New York into a forensic database before procedural television standardized the form.

44. ***The Killer That Stalked New York*** *(1950, Earl McEvoy, USA)* — A jewel smuggler unknowingly carrying smallpox becomes a biological serial killer; the city is mapped through contamination.

45. ***The Sellout*** *(1952, Gerald Mayer, USA)* — Newspaper idealism, local tyranny, and coerced testimony collapse into one small-town corruption machine.

46. ***Without Warning!*** *(1952, Arnold Laven, USA)* — An affectless gardener murders women while the police patiently inventory Los Angeles; unusually dispassionate serial-killer noir.

47. ***The Glass Wall*** *(1953, Maxwell Shane, USA)* — A displaced person wanders nocturnal New York seeking the soldier who can validate his existence; immigration bureaucracy becomes existential noir.

48. ***Crime Against Joe*** *(1956, Lee Sholem, USA)* — A blackout-drunk Tucson painter wakes inside a murder accusation; southwestern bohemia becomes a shabby pocket of social unreality.

49. ***Dear Murderer*** *(1947, Arthur Crabtree, UK)* — A cuckolded husband converts his wife’s affairs into an elaborate chain of murders: brittle, literate, profoundly mean.

50. ***The Upturned Glass*** *(1947, Lawrence Huntington, UK)* — James Mason’s surgeon narrates revenge with the clinical calm of a man dissecting his own moral collapse.

51. ***The Interrupted Journey*** *(1949, Daniel Birt, UK)* — An adulterer reverses his decision to flee, after which guilt appears to rupture time and possibly cause its own disaster.

52. ***The Long Dark Hall*** *(1951, Reginald Beck and Anthony Bushell, UK)* — Fog, pubs, lodging houses, and marital shame create an unusually intimate British city-noir.

53. ***The Last Page / Man Bait*** *(1952, Terence Fisher, UK)* — Bookshop lust becomes blackmail inventory, with Diana Dors turning male embarrassment into a liquid commodity.

54. ***The House Across the Lake / Heat Wave*** *(1954, Ken Hughes, UK)* — A disreputable writer walks willingly into a bargain-basement *Double Indemnity* whose cheapness increases its sordidness.

55. ***The Brain Machine*** *(1955, Ken Hughes, UK)* — An EEG begins as scientific spectacle and becomes a criminal divination device; machinery and psychosis blur together.

56. ***The Man in the Back Seat*** *(1961, Vernon Sewell, UK)* — Two thieves drive through the night with an unconscious bookmaker; every mishap tightens a 57-minute mobile coffin.

57. ***La Dame d’onze heures / The Eleven O’Clock Woman*** *(1948, Jean Devaivre, France)* — Reversals, slow motion, direct address, and montage make postwar noir feel like an experimental construction kit.

58. ***Identité judiciaire*** *(1951, Hervé Bromberger, France)* — Curare attacks and forensic routines transform the Quai des Orfèvres into a clinical factory for manufacturing certainty.

59. ***Des femmes disparaissent / The Road to Shame*** *(1959, Édouard Molinaro, France)* — Human trafficking, Robert Hossein, and Art Blakey: hard French noir driven by jazz percussion rather than languor.

60. ***Un témoin dans la ville / Witness in the City*** *(1959, Édouard Molinaro, France)* — A murderer hunts a taxi driver while the cab-radio network turns Paris into a surveillance organism.

61. ***Le Monte-charge / Paris Pick-Up*** *(1962, Marcel Bluwal, France)* — Duplicate apartments and an elevator sustain an architectural confidence trick of almost mathematical malice.

62. ***Apartado de correos 1001 / P.O. Box 1001*** *(1950, Julio Salvador, Spain)* — Francoist police orthodoxy cannot conceal the derelict Barcelona leaking through its procedural surface.

63. ***Brigada criminal*** *(1950, Ignacio F. Iquino, Spain)* — Street photography and an unfinished-hospital climax overpower the film’s obligatory law-and-order framing.

64. ***Distrito quinto*** *(1957, Julio Coll, Spain)* — Five thieves wait for the missing man carrying their money: a claustrophobic, fractured *Reservoir Dogs* ancestor.

65. ***A sangre fría*** *(1959, Juan Bosch, Spain)* — A factory robbery succeeds, leaving its conspirators to destroy one another over money, class resentment, and narrative control.

66. ***Cuatro contra el mundo*** *(1950, Alejandro Galindo, Mexico)* — A wounded robbery crew seals itself inside a hideout; criminal solidarity emerges as a fatal class delusion.

67. ***El suavecito*** *(1951, Fernando Méndez, Mexico)* — A pachuco pimp and billiards hustler performs masculinity until the performance acquires its own fatal momentum.

68. ***El hombre sin rostro / The Man Without a Face*** *(1950, Juan Bustillo Oro, Mexico)* — Psychoanalytic serial-killer noir fusing maternal repression, professional failure, dreams, and divided identity.

69. ***Si muero antes de despertar / If I Should Die Before I Wake*** *(1952, Carlos Hugo Christensen, Argentina)* — Cornell Woolrich filtered through childhood fear; adult disbelief becomes the murderer’s protection.

70. ***El crimen de Oribe / The Crime of Oribe*** *(1950, Leopoldo Torre Nilsson and Leopoldo Torres Ríos, Argentina)* — Bioy Casares’s repeated Christmas Eve becomes temporal-gothic noir about imprisoning death inside ritual.

71. ***Amei um Bicheiro / I Loved a Bookie*** *(1952, Jorge Ileli and Paulo Wanderley, Brazil)* — A medical bill sends a reformed gambler back into Rio’s illegal lottery economy; crime arises from liquidity rather than appetite.

72. ***Mãos Sangrentas / Bloody Hands*** *(1955, Carlos Hugo Christensen, Brazil)* — A prison-island escape film shot with brutal tropical expressionism and nearly Soviet montage.

73. ***Intimidation*** *(1960, Koreyoshi Kurahara, Japan)* — A corrupt bank manager is blackmailed into robbing his own bank; corporate and criminal hierarchy become the same machine.

74. ***Smashing the 0-Line*** *(1960, Seijun Suzuki, Japan)* — An amoral reporter manufactures news amid drug and human trafficking; style arrives as ethical abrasion.

75. ***The Sleeping Beast Within*** *(1960, Seijun Suzuki, Japan)* — Heroin traffic, journalism, and a sun cult reveal respectable family identity as organized crime’s domestic disguise.

76. ***Black Hair*** *(1964, Lee Man-hee, South Korea)* — Gangster law, opium, mutilation, rape, and prostitution organized around a patriarch’s need to obey his invented code.

77. ***Lewat Djam Malam / After the Curfew*** *(1954, Usmar Ismail, Indonesia)* — A freedom fighter discovers that the revolution’s beneficiaries converted sacrifice into private enterprise.

78. ***Afsporet / Derailed*** *(1942, Bodil Ipsen and Lau Lauritzen Jr., Denmark)* — An amnesiac bourgeois woman descends into Copenhagen’s criminal underside; startlingly early and sexually fatalistic Danish noir.

79. ***Døden er et kjærtegn / Death Is a Caress*** *(1949, Edith Carlmar, Norway)* — An affair survives divorce and marriage only to reveal that consummated desire is more destructive than forbidden desire.

80. ***Meeuwen sterven in de haven / Seagulls Die in the Harbour*** *(1955, Rik Kuypers, Ivo Michiels and Roland Verhavert, Belgium)* — A murderer wanders Antwerp’s docks; the port becomes a ghostly geography between noir and neorealism.

81. ***Epilog: Das Geheimnis der Orplid / Epilogue*** *(1950, Helmut Käutner, West Germany)* — A mysteriously sunken yacht reveals post-Nazi arms traffic: *The Third Man* refracted through maritime conspiracy and German amnesia.

82. ***Pętla / The Noose*** *(1958, Wojciech Jerzy Has, Poland)* — An alcoholic tries to survive until treatment begins; clocks, rooms, and encounters form a closed fatal system.

83. ***Zbrodniarz, który ukradł zbrodnię / The Criminal Who Stole a Crime*** *(1969, Janusz Majewski, Poland)* — A retired militia captain reopens a solved death; documentary procedure gradually yields to metaphysical exhaustion.

84. ***Buđenje pacova / The Rats Woke Up*** *(1967, Živojin Pavlović, Yugoslavia)* — Belgrade’s black markets, boarding rooms, invalids, and sexual humiliations become Black Wave noir.

85. ***Delo “Pyostrykh” / The Variegateds Case*** *(1958, Nikolai Dostal, USSR)* — Postwar Moscow’s black market survives beneath socialist reconstruction; ideology closes the case but not the moral wound.

---

These are claims, not consensus. Some possess tiny cult constituencies, but none has received the full reappraisal its construction warrants. *The Counselor* is the deliberate control case—you already understand that film correctly.

86. ***The Counselor*** *(2013, Ridley Scott)* — Misread as portentous dialogue attached to nihilistic incident. It is a juridical noir in which language constitutes the contract, unseen decisions are irrevocable, and information always arrives after it can save anyone.

87. ***The Gingerbread Man*** *(1998, Robert Altman)* — Treated as Altman slumming in John Grisham territory. Altman transforms the legal thriller into a humid social ecology where weather, class, testimony, and erotic projection contaminate one another.

88. ***The Tailor of Panama*** *(2001, John Boorman)* — Received as an arch Le Carré comedy. It is an information-market masterpiece: intelligence is fabricated to meet demand, then institutions make the fabrication materially true.

89. ***The Way of the Gun*** *(2000, Christopher McQuarrie)* — Misfiled as post-*Pulp Fiction* macho sadism. It is an extraordinarily exact procedural about surrogacy, bagmen, inherited criminal labor, crossfire geometry, and money nobody can safely possess.

90. ***New Rose Hotel*** *(1998, Abel Ferrara)* — Dismissed as underplotted cyberpunk. Its corporate extraction narrative exists primarily as damaged memory, replayed and re-edited by a man who cannot distinguish intimacy from industrial espionage.

91. ***The Underneath*** *(1995, Steven Soderbergh)* — Even Soderbergh has belittled it as an empty exercise. Its fractured chronology and color compartments anatomize a con man continually revising his own motives.

92. ***Duplicity*** *(2009, Tony Gilroy)* — Marketed and judged as an overcomplicated star romance. It is a corporate espionage noir about whether trust can be demonstrated between two people professionally incapable of producing valid evidence.

93. ***Killing Them Softly*** *(2012, Andrew Dominik)* — Condemned for making its economic allegory too obvious. That obviousness is the form: every gangster mouths market ideology while actual price discovery occurs through beating and murder.

94. ***City of Ghosts*** *(2002, Matt Dillon)* — Critics called it “atmosphere without substance.” Cambodia’s geography is the substance: a jurisdictional void where insurance fraud, foreign capital, paternal authority, and expatriate reinvention converge.

95. ***This World, Then the Fireworks*** *(1997, Michael Oblowitz)* — Dismissed as lurid Jim Thompson camp. Its theatrical perversity is exact: everyone constructs a counterfeit self and then becomes trapped inside the counterfeit’s obligations.

96. ***Fear X*** *(2003, Nicolas Winding Refn)* — Treated as an unresolved failed mystery. It is a recursive noir about surveillance imagery, compulsive interpretation, and the possibility that investigation manufactures the guilt it seeks.

97. ***The International*** *(2009, Tom Tykwer)* — Mistaken for a generic bank-conspiracy vehicle. Its central insight is that the adversary is not a villain but settlement infrastructure; individuals can die while the institution clears the transaction.

98. ***The Limits of Control*** *(2009, Jim Jarmusch)* — Called empty because its codes convey almost no practical information. Precisely: signals circulate as rituals, artworks become operational channels, and the assassin is reconstructed as a spectator.

99. ***The Black Dahlia*** *(2006, Brian De Palma)* — Judged as an incoherent prestige adaptation. Its disjunctions describe Hollywood’s inability to encounter a murdered woman except through casting, duplication, pornography, and fabricated images.

100. ***The Little Things*** *(2021, John Lee Hancock)* — Mistaken for a failed serial-killer throwback. It is an anti-procedural about detectives solving their psychic need for culpability rather than the crime, ending with guilt transferred through counterfeit evidence.
